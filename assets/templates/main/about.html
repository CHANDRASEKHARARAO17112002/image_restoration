    Gaussian Noise

    Gaussian noise follows a normal distribution, causing random variations in pixel intensity. It typically occurs due to electronic circuit noise, thermal noise in sensors, or transmission errors. This noise affects all pixels randomly and is more noticeable in high ISO images. Common methods to reduce Gaussian noise include Gaussian smoothing filters, Wiener filtering, and deep learning-based denoising.

    Salt and Pepper Noise (Impulse Noise)

    Salt and pepper noise appears as random black (0) and white (255) pixels scattered across an image. It is usually caused by transmission errors, sensor malfunctions, or sudden disturbances. This noise does not affect neighboring pixels, making it easier to filter out using median filtering, adaptive filtering, and morphological operations. Uniform noise occurs when all intensity values within a certain range have an equal probability of appearing in the image. It is usually caused by quantization errors or hardware malfunctions.

    Poisson Noise (Shot Noise)

    Poisson noise follows a Poisson distribution, where the variance is proportional to pixel intensity. It commonly occurs in low-light conditions due to photon fluctuations and is often found in astrophotography and medical imaging. Techniques like wavelet denoising and variance-stabilizing transformations, such as the Anscombe transform, help reduce Poisson noise.
